WHO NEEDS MY BLOOD ?

  • Individuals with certain blood disorders or diseases, such as sickle cell anemia, may need periodic transfusions to alleviate symptoms and improve overall health.
  • Women experiencing complications during childbirth, such as postpartum hemorrhage, may need 2-3 units of blood to address excessive bleeding.
  • Patients undergoing specific medical treatments, such as chemotherapy or radiation therapy for cancer, may require blood transfusions to manage the side effects and complications associated with these treatments.

    • CAN I DONATE BLOOD ?

  • Donors typically need to be 16-70 years old, with variations based on local regulations and blood donation centers.
  • Donors must be in good health, free from acute illnesses, recent surgeries, or medical conditions that affect donation safety.
  • Donors should meet a minimum weight of 49kg, ensuring sufficient blood volume, and maintain healthy hemoglobin levels for eligibility.

    HOW DONATIONS WORK ?

  • Blood donations involve voluntary contributions of blood from individuals who willingly donate to help others in need.
  • Blood donations are collected and managed by specialized organizations and blood banks. These entities ensure a safe and adequate blood supply for medical treatments and emergencies.
  • Blood donations are needed regularly to maintain a sufficient and diverse blood supply. Donors are encouraged to contribute regularly to meet ongoing medical demands and emergencies.

    Thalassemia Awareness

    Type Description
    Major Thalassemia Severe form of thalassemia that requires regular blood transfusions.
    Minor Thalassemia Milder form of thalassemia, often carriers with no or mild symptoms.
    Inheritance Major + Major: All offspring will have Major Thalassemia.
    Major + Minor: 50% chance of having Major Thalassemia, 50% chance of being a carrier (Minor).
    Minor + Minor: 25% chance of having Major Thalassemia, 50% chance of being a carrier (Minor), 25% chance of having no thalassemia.
